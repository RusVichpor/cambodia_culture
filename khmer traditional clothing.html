Khmer Traditional Clothing

(Funan, 1st–6th century)

Simple wrapped cloth, pre-sampot style,Honors the early foundations of Khmer civilization and cultural ingenuity.

(Chenla, 6th–9th century)

Wrapped cloth with belts, simple ornamentation Celebrates growing artistry and respect for social hierarchy.

(Angkor, 9th–15th century)

Sampot chang kben, short top, armlets, crown (mokot) Showcases classical Khmer elegance; foundation for Apsara dance and ceremonial attire.

(Chaktomuk, 15th–17th century)

Sampot, simpler decoration Reflects adaptability, daily life, and continued cultural respect post-Angkor.

(Longvek, 16th–17th century)

Embroidered sampot, influence from Siam/Vietnam Highlights refinement and cosmopolitan taste of the royal courts.

(Oudong, 17th–19th century)

Silk sampot, jewel belts, ceremonial headdresses Symbolizes royal prestige and sophisticated Khmer artistry.

(French Colonial, 1863–1953)

Khmer traditional with Western elements,Demonstrates cultural resilience and harmonious modernization.

(Khmer Wedding Attire, Modern 20th–21st century)

Embroidered silk sampot, crowns, sashes,Represents blessings, prosperity, and respect for tradition in contemporary society.
